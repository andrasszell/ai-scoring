# White Paper Plan

## AI Adoption Intelligence: A Probabilistic Framework for Measuring Corporate AI Depth

---

## 1. Executive Thesis

### Purpose of this section

Open with a clear, ambitious claim.

### Core message

AI adoption is becoming one of the most important hidden variables in company performance, but it is poorly measured. Current approaches over-rely on keyword counts, press releases, surveys, and marketing language. This white paper proposes a mathematical and evidence-grounded framework for estimating the depth of AI adoption inside companies using publicly observable signals.

### Key points

* AI depth is not directly observable.
* Public evidence is noisy, incomplete, duplicated, and strategically shaped.
* The right framing is not simple counting, but probabilistic inference.
* The platform estimates latent AI maturity from multiple evidence streams.
* The output is not only a score, but a score with uncertainty, evidence, and explanation.

### Desired reader reaction

“This is not a dashboard. This is an intelligence methodology.”

---

## 2. The Measurement Problem

### Purpose of this section

Explain why measuring AI adoption is hard.

### Core message

The central challenge is that companies generate many AI-related signals, but those signals do not directly reveal actual AI integration.

### Problems to define

* AI marketing is not AI adoption.
* AI experimentation is not operational dependence.
* AI product announcements are not necessarily AI-native capability.
* Large companies naturally produce more evidence than small companies.
* Some companies disclose heavily; others are silent.
* Public evidence is duplicated across press releases, media articles, job posts, and investor materials.
* Sector language differs significantly.

### Important distinction

The platform must distinguish between:

* AI publicity
* AI awareness
* AI experimentation
* AI workflow adoption
* AI operational integration
* AI productization
* AI-native capability
* AI-first business models

### Mathematical framing

The observed data is not the target. The target is a latent state.

```text
Observed Evidence ≠ AI Depth

Observed Evidence → Inference Process → Estimated AI Depth
```

## 3. The Latent Variable Framework

### Purpose of this section

Introduce the mathematical foundation.

### Core message

AI adoption should be modeled as a hidden state vector.

### Proposed state vector

```text
AI_State = [
    Awareness,
    Usage,
    Integration,
    Productization,
    Operations,
    Research
]
```

### Define each dimension

#### Awareness

Does the company understand AI strategically?

#### Usage

Are employees using AI tools?

#### Integration

Is AI embedded into workflows?

#### Productization

Does the company sell or expose AI-powered products?

#### Operations

Do internal business processes depend on AI?

#### Research

Is the company creating proprietary AI capability?

### Core inference equation

```text
P(AI_State | Evidence)
```

### Why this matters

This structure lets the system distinguish a company that talks about AI from one that depends on AI.

## 5. Evidence Ingestion and Normalization Pipeline

### Purpose of this section

Explain the data process from raw sources to structured evidence.

### Pipeline

```text
Company Universe
    ↓
Source Collection
    ↓
Document Storage
    ↓
Text Extraction
    ↓
Candidate Evidence Retrieval
    ↓
Evidence Classification
    ↓
Source Credibility Weighting
    ↓
Novelty Deduplication
    ↓
Latent Dimension Scoring
    ↓
Uncertainty Estimation
    ↓
Final AI Depth Score
```

### Key engineering principles

* Every score must be traceable to evidence.
* Raw documents and extracted evidence should be stored separately.
* Collection, scoring, and audit should be decoupled.
* The system must support re-scoring when models or weights improve.
* Evidence should be timestamped.
* Scores should be reproducible.

### Data objects

#### Company

Ticker, name, sector, industry, CIK, aliases, subsidiaries.

#### Document

Source type, URL, filing date, title, raw path.

#### Evidence item

Text snippet, source, date, pillar, confidence, metadata.

#### Evidence assessment

Dimension scores, credibility, novelty, marketing risk, explanation.

#### Company score

Dimension scores, final score, uncertainty, classification, evidence coverage.

## 6. Evidence Classification Layer

### Purpose of this section

Show how AI assistance enters the system intelligently.

### Core message

LLMs should not simply generate final scores. They should classify evidence into structured, auditable intermediate variables.

### Classification task

For each evidence item, estimate:

```json
{
  "awareness": 0.0,
  "usage": 0.0,
  "integration": 0.0,
  "productization": 0.0,
  "operations": 0.0,
  "research": 0.0,
  "confidence": 0.0,
  "marketing_risk": 0.0,
  "evidence_strength": 0.0,
  "explanation": ""
}
```

### Important design principle

The LLM is not the scoring system. The LLM is an evidence parser and semantic classifier.

### Why this is important

This preserves interpretability, allows model replacement, supports audits, and prevents black-box scoring.

## 7. Source Credibility Model

### Purpose of this section

Introduce a formal reliability model.

### Core message

Different evidence sources have different signal value.

### Example credibility hierarchy

* Patents: high credibility for research and proprietary capability
* Research papers: high credibility for technical depth
* Technical documentation: high credibility for productization
* GitHub: high credibility for engineering behavior
* Earnings calls: medium-high credibility for strategic intent
* Job postings: medium credibility for investment direction
* Company blogs: medium credibility
* Marketing pages: low-to-medium credibility
* Press releases: low-to-medium credibility unless corroborated

### Proposed scoring modifiers

```text
effective_signal =
raw_evidence_strength
× source_credibility
× recency_weight
× novelty_weight
× specificity_weight
× confidence
```

### Important insight

A single highly specific technical document can be more valuable than 50 generic AI marketing mentions.

## 8. Novelty and Duplication Control

### Purpose of this section

Address a major weakness of naive evidence counting.

### Core message

The system should score unique claims, not repeated mentions.

### Problem

One product launch can produce:

* a press release
* several blog posts
* many news articles
* investor references
* job posts
* partner pages

Without deduplication, the system overestimates AI depth.

### Solution

Cluster evidence by semantic similarity.

### Claim-level representation

```text
canonical_claim
source_count
source_diversity
first_seen_date
last_seen_date
novelty_cluster_id
```

### Scoring rule

Repeated evidence should increase confidence slightly, but should not multiply the adoption score linearly.

## 9. Mathematical Scoring Framework

### Purpose of this section

Present the core quantitative model.

### Core message

The final score is a weighted function of latent dimension estimates, adjusted for confidence and uncertainty.

### Dimension score

For each company and each dimension:

```text
DimensionScore_d =
Aggregate(
    evidence_strength
    × source_credibility
    × recency_weight
    × novelty_weight
    × confidence
)
```

### Final score

```text
AI_SCORE =
w1 × Awareness
+ w2 × Usage
+ w3 × Integration
+ w4 × Productization
+ w5 × Operations
+ w6 × Research
```

### Suggested baseline weights

```text
Awareness:        10%
Usage:            15%
Integration:      25%
Productization:   20%
Operations:       20%
Research:         10%
```

### Important qualification

Weights should be sector-adjusted.

For software companies, productization and research may matter more.

For industrial companies, operations and integration may matter more.

For banks, governance, automation, risk modeling, and internal workflow integration may matter more.

## 10. Uncertainty Estimation

### Purpose of this section

Make the score scientifically honest.

### Core message

The platform should never output only a point estimate. It should output a score with uncertainty.

### Example

```text
AI Depth Score: 72 ± 8
```

### Sources of uncertainty

* Sparse evidence
* Low source credibility
* High marketing risk
* Old evidence
* Conflicting evidence
* Sector disclosure differences
* Heavy duplication
* Weak source diversity

### Confidence increases when

* Multiple independent source types agree
* Evidence is recent
* Evidence is specific
* Evidence is technical or operational
* Evidence maps consistently to several dimensions

### Confidence decreases when

* Evidence is generic
* Evidence is purely promotional
* Evidence appears only once
* Evidence is only keyword-based
* Evidence is not operationally specific

## 11. Sector Normalization

### Purpose of this section

Prevent unfair cross-sector comparisons.

### Core message

AI depth should be interpreted relative to sector context.

### Why sector normalization matters

A bank, pharmaceutical company, semiconductor company, retailer, and software company will expose AI adoption differently.

### Proposed outputs

* Raw AI Depth Score
* Sector-adjusted percentile
* Sector-relative dimension scores
* Evidence coverage percentile
* Disclosure intensity score

### Example

```text
Company A
Raw AI Score: 61
Sector Percentile: 88th
Classification: AI Integrated
Evidence Coverage: Medium
Uncertainty: ±10
```

## 12. Classification System

### Purpose of this section

Translate scores into intuitive categories.

### Proposed classes

```text
0–20    AI Minimal
20–40   AI Exploratory
40–60   AI Adopting
60–80   AI Integrated
80–90   AI-Native Operations
90–100  AI-First
```

### Important note

Classification should depend not only on total score, but also on dimension pattern.

### Example

A company with high awareness and high productization but low operations may be an “AI marketer/product experimenter,” not truly AI-integrated.

## 13. Evidence Cards and Auditability

### Purpose of this section

Show that the platform is explainable.

### Core message

Every company score should be supported by a human-readable evidence card.

### Evidence card should include

* Final score
* Uncertainty
* Classification
* Dimension breakdown
* Top supporting evidence
* Contradictory or weak evidence
* Source diversity
* Marketing risk
* Disclosure intensity
* Last updated date

### Example output

```text
Company: Example Corp
AI Depth Score: 74 ± 7
Classification: AI Integrated

Strongest dimensions:
- Productization
- Integration
- Operations

Weakest dimension:
- Research

Top evidence:
1. Product documentation showing AI assistant embedded into workflow.
2. Earnings-call discussion of AI-driven operational savings.
3. Hiring pattern showing MLOps and AI platform roles.
4. Patent activity in machine-learning optimization.
```

## 14. Validation Strategy

### Purpose of this section

Explain how the score will be tested.

### Core message

The methodology must be validated against known company archetypes.

### Validation sets

Create labeled examples of:

* AI marketing-heavy companies
* AI experimenters
* AI adopters
* AI-integrated companies
* AI-native companies
* AI-first companies

### Validation methods

* Expert review
* Pairwise company comparison
* Sector benchmark testing
* Historical backtesting
* Score stability analysis
* Sensitivity analysis
* Evidence ablation tests

### Example ablation test

Remove patents and research papers. Does the company still look AI-native?

Remove marketing pages. Does the score collapse?

Remove job postings. Does the operational signal remain?

## 15. MVP Execution Plan

### Purpose of this section

Connect the white paper to implementation.

### MVP version

The first executable version should include:

```text
Documents
    ↓
LLM Evidence Extraction
    ↓
Credibility Weighting
    ↓
Novelty Deduplication
    ↓
Dimension-Level Scoring
    ↓
Uncertainty Estimate
    ↓
Evidence Card
```

### Explicitly defer

Do not build advanced forecasting, knowledge graphs, causal inference, or dynamic graph systems until the core score is validated.

### MVP success condition

The system should correctly distinguish companies that merely talk about AI from companies that actually integrate AI into products, workflows, operations, and research.

## 16. Strategic Differentiation

### Purpose of this section

End with the intellectual moat.

### Core message

The platform’s differentiation is the combination of mathematical modeling, AI-assisted evidence extraction, and auditable public-data intelligence.

### Differentiators

* Treats AI adoption as a latent variable
* Uses source credibility and uncertainty
* Separates marketing from operational evidence
* Scores dimensions, not just mentions
* Produces auditable evidence cards
* Supports sector normalization
* Can improve over time as evidence and models improve
* Combines human theoretical insight with machine-scale evidence processing

### Final positioning

This is not an AI keyword index.

This is a probabilistic intelligence system for estimating the depth of AI transformation inside companies.
